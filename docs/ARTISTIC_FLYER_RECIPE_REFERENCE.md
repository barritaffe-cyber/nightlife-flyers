# Artistic Flyer Recipe Reference

This file captures the design data from the five good flyer samples. Use it before
building or tuning templates. The point is not to copy the surface style. The point is
to reproduce the composition logic that makes the samples feel intentional.

## Core Recipe

Good flyers start with a dominant visual system, not with decorations.

1. Pick the hero first.
   The hero can be a face, body, creature, car, mask, or accessory. It must own the
   poster before text is added.

2. Build the title as a physical layer.
   The main title is not plain text sitting on top. It is a shape in the composition:
   cropped, overlapped, embedded, textured, glowing, masked, or partially hidden.

3. Use controlled depth.
   Required stack: `background -> ghost/title structure -> subject -> foreground
   title/accent -> metadata lanes -> grade/texture`.

4. Make one focal system.
   The subject and title must work as one object. Metadata, icons, QR codes, offers,
   and dates are supporting lanes only.

5. Compose with value before color.
   The layout must read in black, white, and gray at thumbnail size. Color is the mood,
   not the structure.

6. Restrict text zones.
   Good samples use a few disciplined zones: top brand/date, main title system, side
   utility lanes, bottom offer/location. They do not scatter copy everywhere.

7. Integrate texture.
   Texture must bind the whole flyer: background grain, type distress, glow, smoke,
   scratches, blur, or vignette. Texture is not a last-minute filter.

8. Preserve designed negative space.
   Empty or quiet areas are part of the composition. Do not fill every gap.

## Hard Anti-Patterns

- Do not mix unrelated visual languages, such as a photographic subject over a busy
  cartoon crowd, unless the whole recipe is built around that collision.
- Do not use a busy illustrated scene as the hero when the design needs a subject-led
  poster.
- Do not let crowd, car, subject, title, script, price, and background compete at the
  same strength.
- Do not center everything and call it composed.
- Do not paste clean vector UI elements over cinematic art without aging, texture, or
  integration.
- Do not use effects to rescue weak hierarchy.
- Do not add more lanes because the composition feels empty. Fix the hero/title system.

## Sample 01: Elite Fantasy Portrait

Reference: `sample01.jpg`

### What It Is

A fantasy portrait flyer where the face is the poster and all copy supports the central
character.

### Composition Recipe

- Hero: one large fantasy face, cropped tall, centered, and lit with blue/pink contrast.
- Focal point: glowing eyes, lips, and floral headpiece.
- Main title: large chrome/white block title across the lower portrait/chest zone.
- Script accent: attached directly to the title, not floating separately.
- Support lanes: logo top, red date bar near top, music credit under title, red offer
  band, venue footer.
- Depth: portrait/background is one atmospheric world; title sits in front but inherits
  glow and haze from the scene.

### Required Moves

- Protect the face and eyes.
- Place the title low enough to avoid fighting the face.
- Use red bars as structural anchors, not random badges.
- Keep the bottom dense but aligned.

### Avoid

- Shrinking the portrait into a background.
- Adding unrelated stickers around the face.
- Letting the offer band overpower the title.

## Sample 02: Cinematic Creature Cover

Reference: `sample02.jpg`

### What It Is

A full cinematic cover composition where creature, face, title, side lanes, and footer
belong to one dark fantasy world.

### Composition Recipe

- Hero: stacked creature head above human face.
- Focal point: glowing eyes and creature mouth.
- Main title: rendered graphic embedded between creature and face.
- Side lanes: vertical date on left, vertical time on right.
- Footer: DJ, offer copy, event name, and sponsor logos in a narrow bottom stack.
- Foreground depth: leaves, particles, blur, and vignette frame the face.

### Required Moves

- Build as one cinematic image first.
- Treat the title as a custom graphic layer.
- Use symmetrical pressure without making every detail equal.
- Keep side lanes secondary and high-contrast.

### Avoid

- Building this from random creature stickers.
- Using normal editable text for the main title without graphic treatment.
- Flattening the creature and face into equal background noise.

## Sample 03: Luxury Mask Event Layout

Reference: `sample03.jpg`

### What It Is

A luxury event layout where the accessory/portrait subject anchors the bottom half and
the title system owns the upper half.

### Composition Recipe

- Hero: lower-center portrait with mask/glasses as the visual object.
- Title: elegant geometric title plus script, centered in the upper third.
- Date row: horizontal date/time line crossing the composition before the subject.
- Ghost type: subtle large background text behind the subject.
- Offer/footer: framed offer band and dense venue footer, locked to the bottom.
- Background: dark venue/luxury environment with purple and emerald lighting.

### Required Moves

- Keep the top brand cluster separate from the title.
- Use a clean horizontal divider for date/time authority.
- Let the subject overlap the ghost title.
- Put dense logistics in a bottom lane only.

### Avoid

- Random date placement.
- Crowding the top title with logos.
- Burying RSVP and offer copy without a designed lane.

## Sample 04: Giant Face Vertical Type

Reference: `sample04.jpg`

### What It Is

A bold poster where a giant cropped face and huge stacked type become the same
composition.

### Composition Recipe

- Hero: face cropped so large the head, sunglasses, lips, and earrings become geometry.
- Lens copy: script sits inside the sunglasses as part of the subject.
- Main title: huge stacked vertical block type across the torso/lower face zone.
- Lower field: black base area for support copy.
- Accent lanes: red date card on left, small attractions stack on right, venue footer.
- Color: hot pink/red background, gold title/accessories, black lower anchor.

### Required Moves

- Crop aggressively. The face must not feel like a small portrait.
- Use the main type block as the center structure.
- Attach the title to the subject silhouette.
- Keep side info compact and subordinate.

### Avoid

- Standard horizontal headline placement.
- Making the face small.
- Scattering support copy around the subject.

## Sample 05: Sunday Red Only Subject Poster

Reference: `sample05.jpg`

### What It Is

A subject-led red event poster where the model, giant background title, script title,
and sparse metadata create depth.

### Composition Recipe

- Hero: centered model, upper body visible, cropped large enough to dominate.
- Background title: huge `SUNDAY` behind the subject, cropped and partially hidden.
- Foreground accent: glowing script phrase across the subject/title intersection.
- Side lanes: date on left, music/DJ stack on right, entry/QR in lower-right.
- Bottom lane: offer, drinks/hookah/food, address, small brand mark.
- Color: deep red/black base with white typography and controlled red glow.

### Required Moves

- Put the giant day/title behind the extracted subject.
- Use script as the emotional accent across the focal middle.
- Keep metadata small, aligned, and in lanes.
- Use particles/glow as atmosphere, not as decoration.

### Avoid

- Turning the red palette into flat monochrome.
- Making all text the same brightness.
- Letting QR/price compete with the subject/title.

## Center Hero Template Guidance

For the center hero template, use Sample 05 and the subject-isolation rule as the main
guide. If matching the Marcus/Sanders reference, the recipe is:

1. Use a restrained, low-noise background or a prepared cinematic base.
2. Place the large headline behind the extracted subject.
3. Add a lower-opacity ghost headline behind or below the main headline.
4. Place the subject so it physically occludes the headline.
5. Place the script accent across the subject/title intersection.
6. Keep date, QR, price, side promo, and footer copy in lanes.
7. Add texture/grade after the layer structure is correct.

The bad version failed because it used a bright illustrated crowd as a competing hero,
then pasted a photographic subject and typography on top. The correct recipe is a
subject/title system with supporting lanes, not a collage of nightlife symbols.
